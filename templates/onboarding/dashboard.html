The veteran's survival guide

The things we all wish someone had told us on day one. Nullsec is the best part of EVE — it just doesn't forgive not knowing the rules.

Ships are ammo

  • Fly only what you can lose. If losing the ship would ruin your night, don't undock it. Every hull you fly in null should be one you could replace tomorrow without pain.
  • Losses are tuition, not failure. Every veteran has a killboard full of embarrassing losses; that's how we all learned. Read your loss, pick one thing you'd do differently, and move on.
  • Insurance and SRP soften losses. Buy platinum insurance before you undock (right-click your ship → Insurance) — it pays back a chunk of the hull, never the modules. Fly the corp's doctrine fit (our standard, pre-approved fittings) on an official fleet and the Ship Replacement Program pays you back too — just file your claim after.
  • Keep your combat clone cheap. When your pod dies, the implants in your head die with it. Save the shiny sets for a jump clone — a spare body you can swap into; ask a vet to help you set one up.

Local and d-scan are your radar

  • Watch local chat always. In nullsec everyone in the system shows in local's pilot list the moment they arrive. Keep it visible, and treat any name you don't recognise as a hunter until proven otherwise.
  • Make d-scan a reflex. The directional scanner (default V) shows most ships and probes within ~14 AU — but not cloaked ships or Combat Recon cruisers, so a clear scan is never a promise. Hit it every few seconds while running sites or mining; combat probes on scan means align out and leave now.
  • Watch the intel channels. Pilots report hostiles by system name. Learn the systems around home, so a report three jumps out buys you a full minute of warning instead of none.
  • When in doubt, dock up. Docked, you are truly safe — nobody has ever died docked. Tethered at a friendly structure you're repaired and protected, but you can be bumped off tether and killed, so never go AFK on tether. Stepping away in space? Use safe-logoff, not hope.

Getting around without dying

  • Bubbles catch you in null. Warp disruption bubbles stop your warp early or drag you past a gate into a trap — no warning, no countdown. Inside one you cannot warp at all: turn on your prop mod, burn for the edge (double-click in space away from the wrecks), and warp somewhere else the moment you're clear.
  • Never autopilot in null. Autopilot delivers you to a gate camp like room service. Fly every jump by hand, and check the corp killboard and Gate Camp Watch for recent kills on your route before you commit.
  • Bookmark safes and tacticals. A safe spot (a bookmark made mid-warp, away from anything warpable) gives you somewhere to hide and d-scan from. A tactical is the same idea a few hundred km off a busy gate, so you can look before you leap. Make a few of each in home systems on day one.
  • Your gate cloak buys time. After a jump you stay cloaked up to a minute while you don't move. Never panic-warp — take a breath, read the grid; burning back to the gate is often the right call.

Fleet up: comms and discipline

  • Listen first, talk less. When a fight starts, voice comms belong to the FC and scouts. Your question can wait; a talked-over FC gets people killed.
  • The FC's word is final. A fleet doing one imperfect thing together beats thirty pilots doing their own clever thing separately. Odd order? Follow it, ask why after.
  • Broadcast for reps early. Taking damage? Hit the broadcast in the fleet window — that's how logi (the fleet's healers) know to repair you. Broadcast the type your doctrine tanks: shield fleet, shield button; armor fleet, armor button — the FC will say which. And do it when the shooting starts, not at 10% structure.
  • "X up" means volunteer. When a ping says x up, type an x in the channel the ping names (usually corp chat or the ops channel), ideally with the ship you can bring — you'll get a fleet invite. Never be shy about x-ing up in a newbro doctrine ship; that's exactly what they're for.
  • Never AFK on a gate. The order to jump comes with zero warning; miss it and you're alone in hostile space. Need to step away? Say so on comms first.

Earning ISK where you live

  • Ratting pays in ticks. Bounties from shooting NPC pirates pay out every twenty minutes — a "tick". Part of every bounty is skimmed into the system's ESS bank, and hostiles love raiding it — an ESS warning in intel means get safe like any other hostile report. Start with sites your ship comfortably handles and your ticks grow with your skills.
  • Mine with the fleet. Corp mining fleets come with boosts and many extra eyes on intel — you earn more and die less. A solo miner in null is bait; a fleet miner is an income.
  • PI is easy passive money. Planetary Industry takes minutes of clicking per day once set up and quietly pays millions. Ask in corp chat which planets in our space are good.
  • Use the corp buyback. Hauling loot to Jita through hostile space is how newbros die rich. Sell to the buyback instead: slightly less on paper, zero risk, ISK today.

Ask loud, die less

  • There are no stupid questions. Only silent deaths. Every veteran here asked the exact same questions once — answering them is what the help channels are for.
  • The corp wants you undocked. Nobody is annoyed when a newbro loses a ship on fleet — we're glad you came. Join the next newbro-friendly fleet on the ops board this week; showing up in a cheap doctrine ship is the whole job.
  • Grab a mentor early. Ask in corp chat for a buddy for your first fits, first fleet, first ratting site. One hour with a veteran saves a month of trial and error.
  • Speak up when things break. Tackled (held down by enemy warp jammers so you can't warp off), lost, or confused mid-op? Say so on comms immediately. An FC can often save a pilot who speaks up; nobody can save the one who stays quiet.
