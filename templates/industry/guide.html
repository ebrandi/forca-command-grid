Industry & Economy

How EVE industry works

A plain-English tour from blueprint to profit. Read top to bottom the first time; after that, jump to what you need.

1 · Blueprints: BPO vs BPC

A blueprint is the recipe for building something. There are two kinds:

  • BPO — Blueprint Original. Reusable forever. You buy it once (from CCP-seeded market orders for T1) and can build from it endlessly, and research it to make it more efficient.
  • BPC — Blueprint Copy. A copy with a limited number of runs. Made by copying a BPO, or produced by invention (that's how you get T2/T3 blueprints — there are no T2 BPOs).

2 · Manufacturing, ME and TE

Manufacturing turns materials into the product using a blueprint, in an industry job that takes time and costs a job fee. Two blueprint attributes matter:

  • ME — Material Efficiency (0–10). Each level cuts the materials a job consumes by 1%, up to 10% at ME 10. Higher ME = cheaper builds. Research it on a BPO before mass production.
  • TE — Time Efficiency (0–20). Each level cuts the job time by 1%, up to 20%. It doesn't change materials or cost, only how long the job runs.

The Manufacturing calculator applies ME for you and shows the material bill after efficiency.

3 · Copying and research

Three more blueprint jobs, all run on a BPO:

  • Copying makes BPCs from a BPO (needed as the input to invention).
  • ME research raises Material Efficiency; TE research raises Time Efficiency.

4 · T2 invention

Invention is how you make Tech II. You take a T1 BPC, add datacores (and optionally a decryptor), and run an invention job that might succeed. On success you get a T2 BPC with a few runs.

  • Datacores — consumable science items, spent whether the job succeeds or fails.
  • Decryptor — an optional modifier that trades success chance for more runs / better ME / TE on the resulting BPC.
  • Probability — the chance a single job succeeds. It rises with your science + encryption skills and the decryptor. If a job has a 34% chance, you'll average about 3 attempts per success — so the real cost per T2 BPC includes the datacores you burned on the failures.

The T2 invention planner does this maths — probability, expected attempts, and cost per successful BPC — and tells you whether it's cheaper to invent, buy the BPC, or just buy the finished T2 item.

5 · What a job costs

Building isn't just materials. A manufacturing job also charges an installation fee based on:

  • Estimated Item Value (EIV) — CCP's reference value of the materials.
  • System cost index — how busy that solar system's industry is (busier = pricier).
  • Facility tax — set by whoever owns the structure you build in.

Our calculator estimates the fee from CCP adjusted prices × the cost index + facility tax. Structure and rig bonuses aren't modelled, so treat it as a close guide.

6 · Profit, honestly

Your profit is what you sell for, minus everything it took to make and move it:

net profit = sale price − sales tax − broker fee − materials − job fee − invention − hauling

  • Sales tax and broker fee are taken when you sell on the market.
  • Margin is profit as a % of the sale price; break-even is the lowest price you can sell at without losing ISK.

Every number this tool shows is an estimate from current Jita prices and your assumptions — not guaranteed profit. Prices move. Always sanity-check before committing ISK.

7 · Working with the corp

Industry keeps the fleet flying. The corp needs a steady supply of doctrine hulls and modules; the production plans board and the Corp Store demand let leadership say "we need N of X", and members can claim those builds. When you import your ESI jobs, leadership can see what's already in production so nobody double-builds.

Jargon, fast

BPO / BPC
Blueprint Original (reusable) / Copy (limited runs).
ME / TE
Material / Time Efficiency of a blueprint.
Datacore
Consumable science item for invention.
Decryptor
Optional invention modifier (chance ↔ runs/ME/TE).
EIV
Estimated Item Value — the job-fee base.
Cost index
Per-system industry busyness that scales job fees.
Reaction
A job that combines materials into intermediate goods (e.g. alloys, fuel).
Margin
Profit as a percentage of the sale price.
